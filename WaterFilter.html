Hollow Fiber Membrane Water Filter

Sustainable mechanical filtration system designed to prevent toxic blue-green algae proliferation in treatment ponds. Developed for engineering applications in Tampakan, Philippines[cite: 1].

Project Overview

This project involved designing a hollow fiber membrane water filter specifically to combat the growth of toxic cyanobacteria in equatorial climates[cite: 1]. The system utilizes natural Ramie fibers to create a porous filtration medium that traps contaminating particles while maintaining high flow rates and low energy consumption[cite: 3, 9].

The Problem

Nutrient-rich water in treatment ponds often leads to the proliferation of cyanotoxins like microcystin, which pose severe health risks to livestock and reduce plant efficiency[cite: 1]. Traditional methods like reverse osmosis are often too costly for large-scale deployment and risk frequent filter failure[cite: 3].

My Role – Material & Design Lead

  • Conducted material selection using ANSYS Granta EduPack to identify high-performance sustainable fibers [cite: 8]
  • Calculated critical engineering metrics including an effective Young’s Modulus of 41.6 GPa and Yield Strength of 257 MPa [cite: 9]
  • Optimized filter porosity to 0.305 with a micropore radius of 0.75 µm to effectively trap algae [cite: 9]
  • Performed Eco Audits to minimize energy usage during the manufacturing and transport phases [cite: 10]
  • Developed the final assembly concept using 9.214g of Ramie fiber per unit to meet cost-efficiency goals [cite: 10]

Design Process

  • Research: Evaluated Philippine DENR water quality standards and contaminant profiles [cite: 1, 5]
  • Selection: Compared Ramie, Coir, and Sisal fibers based on hydrophilicity and mechanical performance [cite: 8, 9]
  • Analysis: Utilized Material Performance Index (MPI) and decision matrices to justify material choice [cite: 8]
  • Sustainability: Integrated back-washable maintenance features to extend filter lifespan [cite: 7]
  • Final Design: Verified the design met the 1µg/L microcystin concentration limit [cite: 5]

Results & Reflection

The final design achieved a significant reduction in cyanobacteria biomass while keeping fiber costs at approximately $0.09 per unit[cite: 10]. The use of Ramie fiber provided a high-density filtration path with superior hydrophilicity compared to other natural fibers[cite: 9]. This project highlighted the importance of balancing mechanical performance with environmental regulations and sustainable material life cycles.
